October in the Northeast garden is bittersweet. The tomatoes are done, the first frost has taken the basil, and there's a chill in the morning air that says the season is over. But it's not â€” not quite.

What you do in the next four to six weeks determines how your garden performs next spring. The gardeners who put their beds to sleep properly wake up to workable, fertile soil in April. Everyone else fights compacted, weedy, depleted ground while their neighbors are already planting [peas](/blog/best-vegetables-for-northeast-gardens).

Here's your fall checklist.

## Step 1: Final Harvests and Cleanup

### What to pull immediately after first frost:
- **Tomatoes** â€” harvest everything (see our [Northeast tomato guide](/blog/growing-tomatoes-in-the-northeast)), including green fruit. Ripen indoors in paper bags.
- **Peppers** â€” harvest all. Green peppers are fine for cooking.
- **Basil** â€” already dead. Compost the plants.
- **Beans** â€” pull plants, compost healthy foliage. Leave roots in the ground â€” they've fixed nitrogen.
- **Summer squash and cucumbers** â€” done. Compost.

### What to leave (these handle frost):
- **Kale** â€” harvest through November, December with row cover. Flavor improves.
- **Brussels sprouts** â€” need frost for best flavor. Harvest sprouts bottom-up.
- **Carrots** â€” can stay in the ground under heavy mulch. Dig as needed through early winter.
- **Parsnips** â€” same as carrots. Winter-dug parsnips are remarkably sweet.
- **Leeks** â€” hardy to single digits. Mulch the base heavily and harvest into January in Zone 6â€“7.
- **Spinach** â€” fall-planted spinach under row cover overwinters and produces an early spring crop.

### Cleanup Protocol

Remove all diseased plant material from the garden. Don't compost it â€” bag it and trash it, or burn it if your municipality allows. Late blight, early blight, and Septoria survive in plant debris and will reinfect next year's crops.

Healthy plant material goes to the compost pile. Pull spent crops including roots. Remove stakes, cages, and trellises â€” clean them with a 10% bleach solution before storing.

**Pro Tip:** Don't leave bare soil over winter. Exposed Northeast soil erodes, compacts from rain and snow, and loses organic matter. Cover it â€” with mulch, cover crops, or compost. Something.

## Step 2: Plant Garlic

This is the most important fall garden task. If you do nothing else, plant garlic.

### When to Plant

The rule: **2â€“4 weeks before the ground freezes.** You want roots to establish but not top growth.

- **Zone 4:** Late September to mid-October
- **Zone 5:** Mid to late October
- **Zone 6:** Late October to mid-November
- **Zone 7:** November

### How to Plant

1. **Source quality seed garlic.** Don't plant grocery store garlic â€” it's often treated to prevent sprouting and may be a softneck variety that won't perform in cold climates. Order from a regional grower or seed company.

2. **Break bulbs into individual cloves** the day of planting. Keep the papery wrapper on each clove. Discard tiny interior cloves â€” they'll produce tiny bulbs.

3. **Plant cloves 3 inches deep, 6 inches apart, pointy end up.** Rows 12 inches apart.

4. **Mulch immediately with 4â€“6 inches of straw or shredded leaves.** This insulates the soil, prevents frost heaving, and suppresses spring weeds.

5. **Walk away.** Don't water unless it's bone-dry. Don't check on it. Don't worry. Garlic is the most forgiving crop in the garden.

### Best Garlic Varieties for the Northeast

Hardneck varieties only. They need the cold.

- **'Music'** â€” the Northeast default. Big cloves, easy to peel, stores until March. If you grow one variety, make it this.
- **'German Extra Hardy'** â€” porcelain type, very cold-tolerant, Zone 4 proven
- **'Chesnok Red'** â€” purple stripe, phenomenal roasted, medium storage
- **'Georgian Fire'** â€” hot raw, mellows beautifully cooked, good in Zone 4â€“5
- **'Spanish Roja'** â€” rocambole type, outstanding raw flavor, shorter storage (use by December)
- **'Inchelium Red'** â€” one of the few softneck varieties that performs in Zone 5â€“6, stores exceptionally well

Order seed garlic by August for October planting. Popular varieties sell out fast.

**Pro Tip:** Save your biggest, best-formed bulbs from this year's harvest to plant next year. After 2â€“3 generations, your garlic is adapted to your specific microclimate and outperforms anything you can buy.

## Step 3: Soil Amendments

Fall is the ideal time to amend soil. Winter freezing and thawing cycles break down organic matter and incorporate amendments naturally.

### Soil Test First

If you haven't tested your soil in the past 2â€“3 years, do it now. Your state's Cooperative Extension runs soil testing labs:

- **UMass** (Massachusetts) â€” $20, excellent recommendations
- **Cornell** (New York) â€” $15 basic test
- **Penn State** (Pennsylvania) â€” $9 basic test
- **UConn** (Connecticut) â€” $12 basic test
- **UVM** (Vermont) â€” $14 basic test

Get results before you add amendments. Random lime and fertilizer applications without a soil test are wasteful at best, harmful at worst.

### Common Fall Amendments

Based on your soil test results:

- **Lime** â€” if pH is below 6.0 (common in the Northeast, where acid rain and conifer needles acidify soil). Fall application gives lime all winter to react. Apply **pelletized limestone** at the rate your soil test recommends â€” typically 25â€“75 lbs per 1,000 sq ft.
- **Compost** â€” 1â€“2 inches spread over beds. The single best all-purpose amendment. Improves drainage in clay, water retention in sand, and feeds soil biology regardless.
- **Aged manure** â€” if you have access. Apply in fall only (not spring â€” too much nitrogen too fast). 1â€“2 inches worked into the top few inches.
- **Wood ash** â€” if you have a woodstove. Raises pH (use cautiously â€” less than 5 lbs per 100 sq ft) and adds potassium. Don't use if pH is already above 6.5.
- **Rock phosphate or bone meal** â€” only if your soil test shows phosphorus deficiency. Northeast soils are often adequate in phosphorus.

**Pro Tip:** Compost is never wrong. If you're unsure about what your soil needs and can't test this fall, just add compost. Two inches. Every year. You'll build amazing soil within three seasons.

## Step 4: Cover Crops

Cover crops are the single biggest upgrade most Northeast home gardeners aren't using. They prevent erosion, suppress weeds, fix nitrogen (legumes), break up compacted soil, and add organic matter when turned under in spring.

### Best Fall Cover Crops for the Northeast

- **Winter rye** â€” the workhorse. Sow through late October (Zone 5). Germinates in cold soil, survives any Northeast winter, extensive root system breaks up compaction. Turn under in spring 2â€“3 weeks before planting. **Caution:** once it heads out in spring, it's hard to kill without a lot of effort. Mow/turn under early.

- **Crimson clover** â€” nitrogen fixer. Beautiful red flowers in spring. Sow by mid-September (Zone 5) â€” it needs to establish before winter. Winter-kills in Zone 4â€“5, making spring bed prep easier.

- **Hairy vetch** â€” aggressive nitrogen fixer. Sow by early October. Survives winter. Fixes 60â€“100 lbs of nitrogen per acre. Excellent before nitrogen-hungry crops like corn or brassicas.

- **Oats** â€” sow by mid-September. Winter-kills reliably in all Northeast zones, leaving a nice mulch layer. The lazy gardener's cover crop â€” no spring management needed.

- **Field peas + oats mix** â€” the best of both worlds. Peas fix nitrogen, oats add biomass. Both winter-kill. Sow by mid-September.

For beds that you won't plant until June (tomato, pepper, squash beds), winter rye is ideal. For beds you want to plant early (peas, lettuce), use oats or the oat/pea mix that winter-kills.

## Step 5: Protect Perennials

### Asparagus
Cut ferns after they've yellowed completely (mid-November). Apply 2â€“3 inches of compost and 4 inches of straw mulch.

### Rhubarb
Remove dead foliage. Top-dress with compost. In Zone 4, add 4â€“6 inches of straw.

### Strawberries
After several hard frosts (plants are dormant), mulch with 4â€“6 inches of clean straw. In Zone 4â€“5, this is critical â€” unprotected strawberry crowns can be killed by deep freezes. Remove mulch gradually in spring when new growth appears.

### Herbs
- **Rosemary** â€” not reliably hardy above Zone 7. Dig and pot it, or take cuttings.
- **Thyme, oregano, chives, sage** â€” hardy to Zone 4â€“5. Mulch lightly. They'll return.
- **Lavender** â€” survives in Zone 5+ with excellent drainage. Mulch lightly with gravel, not organic mulch (which holds moisture against the crown).

## Step 6: Tool and Infrastructure Maintenance

While you're at it:

- **Sharpen and oil tools.** Clean off soil, sharpen hoe and pruner blades, coat metal with a light oil (WD-40 or linseed oil).
- **Drain and store hoses.** A frozen, cracked hose in April is a $40 surprise nobody wants.
- **Clean and store row cover and plastic mulch.** Dry thoroughly before folding.
- **Repair raised beds.** Rotting boards are easier to replace now than in the spring mud.
- **Service your tiller** if you use one. Oil change, spark plug, blade inspection.
- **Clean the greenhouse/cold frame.** Wash glazing for maximum light transmission through winter.

## The October-November Timeline

Here's your fall sequence:

**Early October:**
- Final warm-season harvests
- Pull spent plants, clean beds
- Soil test (if due)
- Plant garlic (Zone 4â€“5)
- Sow cover crops (last chance for most varieties)

**Mid-October:**
- Apply lime and amendments
- Plant garlic (Zone 5â€“6)
- Spread compost on all beds
- Mulch carrots and parsnips for in-ground storage

**Late Octoberâ€“Early November:**
- Plant garlic (Zone 6â€“7)
- Mulch perennial beds
- Final kale, Brussels sprout, leek harvests
- Tool maintenance
- Drain hoses, winterize irrigation

**Mid-November:**
- Mulch strawberries after hard freeze
- Bring in rosemary
- Put feet up

## Common Mistakes

1. **Leaving bare soil over winter.** Erosion, compaction, weed seeds. Cover everything.
2. **Composting diseased plant material.** Home compost piles rarely reach temperatures needed to kill pathogens. Trash it.
3. **Skipping garlic planting.** It takes 10 minutes, costs $15 in seed garlic, and produces $50+ worth of garlic with zero effort from November to July.
4. **Liming without a soil test.** Over-liming is a real problem. Soil pH above 7.0 locks out micronutrients. Test first.
5. **Ignoring cover crops.** If it feels like too much, just scatter winter rye seed on your emptied beds and rake it in. Done.
